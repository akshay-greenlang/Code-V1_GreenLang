Part 1 - CTO Final Pathway
This final product strategy deliberately avoids a 36-products-at-once failure mode. GreenLang should be built as one Climate OS substrate with sector applications on top. Product release dates are targets; release gates are binding. A product slips before the substrate is compromised.
Final pathway summary
Final CTO decisions
Keep the full 36-product ambition, but enforce phased release gates. The calendar is the ambition; the gates are the actual ship authority.
Stagger FY27 GA. Factors, Connect, Climate Ledger, SDK/API/CLI, CBAM, and Scope Engine can reach GA in FY27 Q4. Entity Graph, Evidence Vault, Policy Graph, Agent Runtime + Eval, and Comply should GA across FY28 Q1-Q2 because they require stronger assurance, policy, security, and workflow validation.
Treat Comply/CBAM/Scope Engine as the beachhead, not the identity. The long-term identity is system of record, system of action, and system of trust for climate workflows.
Build SupplierOS, PCF Studio, and DPP Hub as one data-network loop. Do not build three unrelated apps.
Gate PlantOS and BuildingOS on measurable savings and repeatable integrations. Their job is CFO-grade ROI, not dashboard theater.
Launch finance, MRV, and markets only after the trust layer is credible. Do not touch take-rates or transaction brokerage without legal role clarity.
Incubate public-sector, nature, CDR, and circularity with anchor customers; do not let late-horizon products consume FY27-FY28 core engineering bandwidth.
Non-negotiable release gates

AI governance model
AI agents may map messy activity data, summarize policy, draft supplier messages, triage evidence gaps, generate scenario narratives, and prepare first-draft reports.
AI agents must never silently create regulated, financial, MRV, or assurance claims. High-stakes outputs require citations, evaluation, reviewer approval, and ledger traceability.
Every agent release must include a pinned model, prompt version, tool-permission profile, eval suite, failure-handling path, and cost guardrail.

Dependency rules
Core Platform: Proceed immediately. Build this as shared infrastructure, not customer-specific services. Every sector cloud must use these shared objects and APIs; no product may fork the factor, entity, evidence, policy, or ledger model.
Compliance Cloud: Proceed as the first commercial wedge. Keep the initial surface narrow, revenue-backed, and assurance-ready. Use Comply, CBAM, and Scope Engine to prove the data model, paid demand, and customer onboarding path.
Supply Chain Cloud: Proceed after the compliance wedge creates supplier and product-data demand. Treat supplier, product carbon, DPP, and proof workflows as one compound loop, not separate business lines.
Operations Cloud: Proceed only where operational data is available and the customer can quantify savings. These products must create CFO-grade ROI; delay any module that behaves like bespoke consulting rather than repeatable software.
Mobility Cloud: Proceed through data-rich shippers, forwarders, and fleets. Do not become generic fleet or TMS software; win on verified emissions, transition planning, and embedded logistics-carbon APIs.
Land/Water/Nature: Incubate with anchor customers or consortiums, then productize. These domains have high mission value but high data and methodology risk; release decision-support first and verified claims only after evidence and methodology review.
Risk & Public Sector: Proceed after the Entity Graph and asset/site data are mature. Keep early risk outputs explainable and scenario-based. Public-sector products should require paid pilots or funded deployments, not free civic demos.
Finance & Markets: Proceed after the trust substrate is credible. Keep analytics separate from regulated financial advice, require legal review for transaction/take-rate flows, and sequence MRV before marketplaces or CDR portfolios.
Circularity: Launch late and tie tightly to DPP, PCF, SupplierOS, and evidence data. Treat circularity as a product-lifecycle extension, not a new disconnected category.

12. Cross-product dependency notes
Three dependency patterns matter for sequencing decisions:
3.1 Substrate before sector clouds
Factors, Connect, Entity Graph, Climate Ledger, Evidence Vault, and Policy Graph must all ship to v0.9 Public Beta before any sector cloud goes Public Beta. Skipping this discipline is how climate-tech companies end up with 30 disconnected products. The sequencing pain is real in FY27 (revenue is slower), but the architectural payoff is every subsequent product.
3.2 The supply-chain / compliance loop
Comply is the wedge that opens the door. CBAM and Scope Engine make the wedge concrete for manufacturers. SupplierOS, PCF Studio, and DPP Hub close the loop by turning supplier responses into structured data that flows back into Ledger and Comply. Expect most FY27-FY28 ACV to come from this loop before operations, risk, or finance become material.
3.3 Operations outcomes feed platform stickiness
PlantOS, BuildingOS, DataCenter CarbonOps, PowerOS, and FlexOS are the ROI-grade products that get CFO attention. Once the Ledger has verified savings flowing into it, the platform is no longer a reporting tool - it is the system of record for climate operations and the substrate on which RiskOS, FinanceOS, and Markets products stand.
3.4 The finance-and-markets spine
MRV Studio (FY29), FinanceOS (FY29), Transition Finance Studio (FY30), Carbon Markets Hub (FY30), and CDR Portfolio Manager (FY31) compose the finance-and-markets spine. They are deliberately sequenced after substrate and operations have hardened, because their value proposition is 'trust plus workflow' - and trust requires the substrate to be production-grade first.
3.5 Public-sector and circularity tail
CityOS (FY31) and Circularity Hub (FY31) are the slowest-to-revenue but highest-mission-alignment products. They are viable because by FY31 the substrate is mature, the brand is established, and partner channels (utilities, MDBs, waste-management networks) exist to distribute.
13. Appendix - version labeling in practice
One practical note on version labeling: the milestone labels (v0.1 Alpha, v0.5 Closed Beta, v0.9 Public Beta, v1.0 GA, v2.0, v3.0) are strategic release boundaries. Between them, products ship monthly or bi-weekly minor releases (v1.1, v1.2, v1.3, ...) as normal SaaS iterations. Those minor releases are not enumerated in this document - they are an engineering cadence decision, not a strategic one.
Design-partner commitments should be pinned to strategic releases. A design partner who signs 'available at v0.5 Closed Beta in FY27 Q2' expects a working end-to-end workflow that quarter, not every minor iteration. A customer who signs 'GA in FY28 Q2' expects SLA and priced packaging to be live that quarter.
For regulator- and auditor-facing products (CBAM, Comply, Scope Engine, MRV Studio, Proof Hub, Evidence Vault), v1.0 GA is gated on external attestation (successful regulator submission, audited customer filing, or verifier acceptance) rather than internal readiness. Those gates are called out in each product's version roadmap.
Open-core products (Factors, SDK/API/CLI) follow the same milestone ladder but additionally version the open-source repos via semver independently - the community tier may hit its own v1.0 ahead of the commercial tier's GA, and that is fine as long as the commercial SLA is honest about what the community tier does and does not cover.
