## 1. CSRD Financial Services Scope

### 1.1 Which Financial Institutions Must Comply

The CSRD applies to all large undertakings and all listed companies (except listed micro-enterprises). After the Omnibus simplification (published in the Official Journal of the EU on 2026-02-26), the revised scope is:

| Entity Type | Threshold | Conditions |
|-------------|-----------|------------|
| Large EU Companies | >1,000 employees AND >EUR 450M net turnover | Both conditions must be met |
| Large EU Banks & Credit Institutions | >1,000 employees AND >EUR 450M net turnover | Including large credit institutions regardless of listing |
| Large EU Insurance Undertakings | >1,000 employees AND >EUR 450M net turnover | Including large insurance undertakings regardless of listing |
| Listed SMEs, Small Banks, Captive Insurers | Listed on EU-regulated markets | Subject to ESRS LSME (simplified standards) |
| Non-EU Companies | >EUR 450M net turnover generated in the EU | With at least one EU subsidiary or branch exceeding EUR 80M turnover |
| EU Financial Holding Companies | -- | Fully EXEMPT (subsidiaries may be subject) |

### 1.2 Compliance Timeline (Post-Omnibus)

| Wave | Reporting Year | Publication Year | Who |
|------|---------------|-----------------|-----|
| Wave 1 | FY2024 | 2025 | Large PIEs already under NFRD (incl. large listed banks, insurers >500 employees). Already reporting. |
| Wave 1 (continued) | FY2025 & FY2026 | 2026 & 2027 | Same Wave 1 entities continue. Quick-Fix DA extends phase-ins and defers additional datapoints for FY2025-FY2026 reports. |
| Wave 2 (POSTPONED) | FY2027 | 2028 | Other large companies meeting new thresholds (>1,000 employees AND >EUR 450M turnover). Originally FY2025, postponed by Stop-The-Clock Regulation (April 2025). |
| Wave 3 (POSTPONED) | FY2028 | 2029 | Listed SMEs, small non-complex credit institutions, captive (re)insurers. Originally FY2026. Can opt out until FY2028. |
| Wave 4 | FY2028 | 2029 | Non-EU companies with >EUR 450M EU turnover |

### 1.3 Key Regulatory References

- **Directive (EU) 2022/2464** (CSRD) amending Directive 2013/34/EU
- **Delegated Regulation (EU) 2023/2772** (Set 1 ESRS)
- **Stop-The-Clock Regulation** (April 2025) -- postpones Wave 2 and Wave 3
- **Omnibus I Directive** (Official Journal 2026-02-26) -- raises thresholds, simplifies
- **Quick-Fix Delegated Act** -- extends phase-ins for Wave 1 FY2025-FY2026 reports
- **Revised ESRS** -- expected delegated act adoption by mid-2026, application from FY2027

### 1.4 Penalties for Non-Compliance

Penalties are set at the Member State level but must be "effective, proportionate and dissuasive." Examples:
- **France**: Up to EUR 75,000 fine and/or 5 years imprisonment for obstructing auditor verification
- **Germany**: Administrative fines up to EUR 10M or 5% of total annual turnover
- **Netherlands**: Fines up to EUR 2M for false sustainability statements
- Financial supervisors (ECB, national banking authorities) may additionally impose prudential consequences

## 2. ESRS Sector-Specific Standards

### 2.1 Current Status

EFRAG has been developing sector-specific ESRS for financial institutions through three Financial Institution Advisory Panels (FIAPs):

| Panel | Focus Areas |
|-------|-------------|
| Banking Panel | Credit institutions, financed emissions, GAR/BTAR, credit risk from climate |
| Capital Markets Panel | Asset managers, investment firms, portfolio carbon footprint |
| Insurance Panel | Insurance and reinsurance undertakings, underwriting emissions |

### 2.2 Development Timeline

| Milestone | Date | Status |
|-----------|------|--------|
| EFRAG FIAPs launched | 2023 | Completed |
| Draft sector standards (secretariat level) | 2024-2025 | In progress (paused at TEG/SRB level) |
| Omnibus I proposal to eliminate sector standards | 2025 | Under negotiation |
| EFRAG monitoring IFRS/SASB work in this area | 2025-2026 | Ongoing |
| Final sector standards (if adopted) | TBD | Depends on legislative outcome |

### 2.3 Practical Implications

While sector-specific ESRS for financial institutions are paused, financial institutions must still:

1. Apply **sector-agnostic ESRS** (Set 1) to all material topics
2. Use **PCAF Standard** for financed emissions (referenced in ESRS E1 AR 46)
3. Follow **EBA Pillar 3 ESG ITS** for banking-specific disclosures (GAR, BTAR, transition risk templates)
4. Apply **EIOPA Guidelines** for insurance-specific Solvency II sustainability reporting
5. Follow **SFDR/Taxonomy Regulation** for product-level disclosures

### 2.4 SASB/IFRS S2 Alignment

EFRAG is monitoring IFRS and SASB work. The SASB standards provide sector-specific guidance for:
- **Commercial Banks** (FN-CB): Financed emissions, ESG integration in credit analysis
- **Insurance** (FN-IN): Underwriting emissions, catastrophe risk management
- **Asset Management** (FN-AC): ESG integration in investment management, proxy voting
- **Investment Banking** (FN-IB): ESG integration in advisory, facilitated emissions

## 3. Financed Emissions / PCAF

### 3.1 Overview

The Partnership for Carbon Accounting Financials (PCAF) provides the globally recognized standard for measuring and disclosing GHG emissions associated with financial activities. The Third Edition (December 2025) is the current version.

- **670+ financial institutions** are PCAF signatories (as of November 2025)
- **ESRS E1 AR 46** explicitly references PCAF as the methodology for financed emissions
- **GHG Protocol** Category 15 "Investments" is the classification for financed emissions

### 3.2 PCAF Standard Structure (December 2025)

| Part | Name | Edition | Scope |
|------|------|---------|-------|
| Part A | Financed Emissions | 3rd Edition (Dec 2025) | 10 asset classes |
| Part B | Facilitated Emissions | 1st Edition (2023) | Capital markets activities |
| Part C | Insurance-Associated Emissions | 2nd Edition (Dec 2025) | Underwriting portfolios |
| Supplemental | Avoided Emissions & Forward-Looking Metrics | New (Dec 2025) | Guidance only |

### 3.3 Core Formula

```
Financed Emissions = Attribution Factor x Borrower/Investee Emissions
```

Where:
```
Attribution Factor = Outstanding Amount / (Total Equity + Total Debt)
                   = Outstanding Amount / EVIC
```
(EVIC = Enterprise Value Including Cash, for listed entities)

### 3.4 Asset Class Methodologies (10 classes, Part A 3rd Edition)

| # | Asset Class | Attribution Factor | Notes |
|---|------------|-------------------|-------|
| 1 | **Listed Equity & Corporate Bonds** | Outstanding Amount / EVIC | EVIC = market cap + total debt. Scope 1+2 mandatory, Scope 3 encouraged. |
| 2 | **Business Loans & Unlisted Equity** | Outstanding Amount / (Total Equity + Total Debt) | Use balance sheet data when EVIC unavailable. |
| 3 | **Project Finance** | Outstanding Amount / Total Project Equity + Debt | Project-level emissions (construction + operational). |
| 4 | **Commercial Real Estate** | Outstanding Amount / Property Value at Origination | Energy-based emissions from buildings. |
| 5 | **Mortgages (Residential)** | Outstanding Amount / Property Value at Origination | Floor area-based or EPC-based emissions. |
| 6 | **Motor Vehicle Loans** | Outstanding Amount / Vehicle Value at Origination | Distance-based or fuel-based emissions. |
| 7 | **Sovereign Debt** | Outstanding Amount / GDP(PPP) | Territorial emissions of sovereign. |
| 8 | **Use of Proceeds Structures** | NEW in 2025. Outstanding Amount / Total Use of Proceeds Financing | Ring-fenced project emissions. |
| 9 | **Securitizations & Structured Products** | NEW in 2025. Look-through to underlying assets. | Apply underlying asset class methodology. |
| 10 | **Sub-Sovereign Debt** | NEW in 2025. Outstanding Amount / Sub-Sovereign Budget | Municipal/regional emissions. |
| -- | **Undrawn Loan Commitments** | NEW in 2025 (optional). | Guidance aligned with IFRS S1 & S2. |

### 3.5 PCAF Data Quality Scoring (1-5 scale)

| Score | Quality | Data Source | Description |
|-------|---------|-------------|-------------|
| **1** | Highest | Verified reported emissions | Audited/verified Scope 1+2 from borrower/investee. GHG emissions verified by third party. |
| **2** | High | Unverified reported emissions | Reported but not third-party-verified Scope 1+2. Company-reported data. |
| **3** | Medium | Physical activity data | Emissions estimated from physical activity data (e.g., kWh energy consumed, tonnes of fuel). Region/sector-specific emission factors. |
| **4** | Low | Economic activity data | Emissions estimated from economic activity data (e.g., revenue, assets). Uses revenue-based emission factors (e.g., tCO2e/EUR M revenue). |
| **5** | Lowest | Estimated/proxy | Emissions estimated based on sector averages, region-sector proxies. No company-specific data. |

Each asset class has its own detailed scoring criteria. Financial institutions must report a **weighted data quality score** for their portfolio and disclose what percentage of portfolio falls into each quality tier.

### 3.6 Calculation Example: Listed Equity

```
Company A:
  - FI's investment in Company A = EUR 10M
  - Company A EVIC = EUR 500M
  - Company A Scope 1+2 emissions = 100,000 tCO2e

Attribution Factor = 10M / 500M = 0.02 (2%)

Financed Emissions = 0.02 x 100,000 = 2,000 tCO2e
```

### 3.7 Calculation Example: Mortgage Portfolio

```
Mortgage Portfolio:
  - Outstanding mortgage amount = EUR 200,000
  - Property value at origination = EUR 400,000
  - Building emissions = 5 tCO2e/year (from EPC data)

Attribution Factor = 200,000 / 400,000 = 0.5 (50%)

Financed Emissions = 0.5 x 5 = 2.5 tCO2e/year
```

### 3.8 Mandatory Reporting Elements

For each asset class, financial institutions must disclose:
1. **Absolute financed emissions** (tCO2e) -- Scope 1, Scope 2, and if available Scope 3
2. **Carbon intensity** (tCO2e per EUR M invested or lent)
3. **Data quality score** (weighted average, 1-5 scale)
4. **Percentage of portfolio covered** by the measurement
5. **Methodology description** including emission factor sources
6. **Year-on-year comparison** (from second reporting year onwards)

## 4. Green Asset Ratio (GAR)

### 4.1 Definition

The Green Asset Ratio (GAR) is the principal KPI for credit institutions under the EU Taxonomy Regulation. It measures the proportion of assets financing taxonomy-aligned economic activities as a share of total covered assets.

### 4.2 Legal Basis

- **EU Taxonomy Regulation** (EU) 2020/852, Article 8
- **Disclosures Delegated Act** (EU) 2021/2178 (as amended by Simplification DA 2025)
- **EBA Pillar 3 ESG ITS** (Templates 6-10)

### 4.3 GAR Formula

```
GAR = Taxonomy-Aligned Assets (Numerator) / Total Covered Assets (Denominator)
```

### 4.4 Numerator: Taxonomy-Aligned Assets

The numerator includes on-balance-sheet assets that finance taxonomy-aligned activities:

| Asset Type | Included in Numerator |
|------------|----------------------|
| Loans and advances to NFRD/CSRD companies | Yes, if taxonomy-aligned |
| Debt securities held (NFRD/CSRD counterparties) | Yes, if taxonomy-aligned |
| Equity instruments held (NFRD/CSRD counterparties) | Yes, if taxonomy-aligned |
| Loans collateralized by residential real estate | Yes, if building meets EPC A or top 15% nationally |
| Loans collateralized by commercial real estate | Yes, if building meets NZEB criteria |
| Motor vehicle loans | Yes, if zero-emission vehicle |
| Loans to households for building renovation | Yes, if compliant with TSC |
| Loans to local/regional governments (taxonomy-aligned projects) | Yes |

### 4.5 Denominator: Total Covered Assets

| Asset Type | In Denominator | In Numerator |
|------------|---------------|--------------|
| Loans to NFRD/CSRD companies | Yes | If aligned |
| Loans to non-NFRD EU companies (SMEs) | Yes | No (unless voluntarily reported) |
| Loans to non-EU counterparties | Yes | No (unless voluntarily reported) |
| Sovereign/central bank exposures | **Excluded** | Excluded |
| Trading book assets | **Excluded** | Excluded |
| Derivatives (held for trading) | **Excluded** | Excluded |
| Interbank on-demand loans | **Excluded** | Excluded |
| Cash and cash equivalents | **Excluded** | Excluded |

### 4.6 GAR Breakdown Requirements

Credit institutions must break down the GAR by:
1. **Environmental objective** (6 objectives: CCM, CCA, WTR, CE, PPC, BIO)
2. **Type of counterparty** (financial/non-financial corporates, households, local authorities)
3. **Stock vs. flow** (existing assets vs. new origination in reporting period)
4. **Transitional vs. enabling activities**
5. **Alignment condition** (taxonomy-eligible but not aligned vs. fully aligned)

### 4.7 2025-2026 Changes (Simplification DA)

| Change | Before | After (from 1 Jan 2026) |
|--------|--------|------------------------|
| Denominator scope | Includes all non-CSRD counterparties | Companies not subject to compulsory sustainability reporting excluded from denominator |
| Off-balance sheet | Not required | Required from 2026: templates for fees, commissions, trading book |
| Voluntary numerator | Allowed for SME/non-EU | Encouraged with simplified reporting |
| Phase-in for new objectives | WTR/CE/PPC/BIO phased in | All 6 objectives fully in scope |

### 4.8 Typical GAR Ranges (Observed 2024-2025)

Based on early disclosures from Wave 1 banks:
- **Average GAR**: 3-8% (most large EU banks)
- **Top performers**: 10-15%
- **Including residential mortgages (EPC A)**: Can significantly increase GAR
- **Key challenge**: Denominator dilution from SME/non-EU exposures

## 5. Banking Book Taxonomy Alignment Ratio (BTAR)

### 5.1 Definition

The BTAR extends the GAR to cover assets NOT captured under EU Taxonomy reporting -- specifically exposures to SMEs, non-EU companies, and other entities not subject to NFRD/CSRD. It provides a broader picture of sustainability alignment across the entire banking book.

### 5.2 Legal Basis

- **EBA Pillar 3 ESG ITS** (Implementing Technical Standards on ESG risk disclosures)
- **CRR3** (Capital Requirements Regulation III)
- Mandatory disclosure from reference date 31 December 2024 onward

### 5.3 BTAR Formula

```
BTAR = Taxonomy-Aligned Exposures (expanded numerator) / Banking Book Total (expanded denominator)
```

### 5.4 Key Differences from GAR

| Dimension | GAR | BTAR |
|-----------|-----|------|
| **Counterparty scope** | Only NFRD/CSRD-subject entities | ALL non-financial corporates (including SMEs, non-EU) |
| **Numerator** | Only assets with verified taxonomy data | Includes bank-estimated taxonomy alignment |
| **Denominator** | Covered assets (excl. sovereign, trading) | Entire banking book (incl. derivatives, intangibles, goodwill) |
| **Data source** | Counterparty-reported taxonomy KPIs | Bank internal estimates, proxies, best-effort |
| **Purpose** | Regulatory transparency on verified green assets | Broader sustainability view, engagement incentive |
| **Mandatory from** | FY2023 (Template 7) | 31 Dec 2024 reference date |

### 5.5 BTAR Expanded Denominator Includes

- All GAR denominator assets
- Exposures to EU non-financial corporates not subject to NFRD
- Exposures to non-EU non-financial corporates
- Financial derivatives (swaps, futures, options, forwards)
- On-demand interbank loans
- Cash, commodities
- Intangible assets (including goodwill)

### 5.6 Calculation Approach

Banks must estimate taxonomy alignment for non-CSRD counterparties using:
1. **Sectoral proxies** -- NACE code mapping to taxonomy activities
2. **Internal ESG assessments** -- Bank's own sustainability evaluation
3. **Third-party ESG data** -- From data providers (MSCI, Sustainalytics, etc.)
4. **Client engagement** -- Direct data collection from borrowers
5. **Conservative estimation** -- Default to non-aligned if no data available

## 6. Insurance Underwriting Emissions

### 6.1 PCAF Part C: Insurance-Associated Emissions

The PCAF Standard Part C (2nd Edition, December 2025) provides methodologies for measuring GHG emissions associated with insurance and reinsurance underwriting activities. These are classified as **Scope 3 Category 15** emissions for the insurer.

### 6.2 Key Principle: "Follow the Risk"

Unlike financed emissions which "follow the money," insurance-associated emissions "follow the risk":

```
Insurance-Associated Emissions = Attribution Factor x Insured Entity Emissions
```

Where:
```
Attribution Factor = Premium Share / Total Premium
```

The risk transfer is the basis for attribution, not capital flows.

### 6.3 Covered Lines of Business

| Line of Business | Edition | Attribution Method |
|-----------------|---------|-------------------|
| Commercial Motor | 1st Edition (2022) | Premium-based, distance-based, fuel-based |
| Personal Motor | 1st Edition (2022) | Premium-based, vehicle-specific |
| Commercial Property | 2nd Edition (2025) | Premium-based, building energy data |
| Commercial General Liability | 2nd Edition (2025) | Revenue-based attribution |
| **Project Insurance** | **NEW 2nd Edition (2025)** | All-risk policies for construction/erection projects |
| **Treaty Reinsurance** | **NEW 2nd Edition (2025)** | Ceded portfolio emissions attribution |

### 6.4 Motor Insurance Calculation (Example)

```
Personal Motor Portfolio:
  - Annual premium collected = EUR 500/vehicle
  - Total market premium for vehicle segment = EUR 1,000
  - Vehicle annual emissions = 3.5 tCO2e

Attribution Factor = 500 / 1,000 = 0.5

Insurance-Associated Emissions per policy = 0.5 x 3.5 = 1.75 tCO2e
```

### 6.5 Data Quality Scoring (Part C)

Same 1-5 scale as Part A, but adapted for insurance context:

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 1 | Verified emissions of the insured (highest quality) |
| 2 | Unverified reported emissions of the insured |
| 3 | Physical activity data of insured (e.g., km driven, energy use) |
| 4 | Economic activity-based estimates (revenue, premium as proxy) |
| 5 | Sector/regional averages, no entity-specific data (lowest quality) |

### 6.6 Reinsurance Specifics (New 2025)

Treaty reinsurers must:
1. Identify the **ceding company's portfolio** underlying the treaty
2. Apply the **cession ratio** as the attribution factor
3. Calculate emissions from the underlying risks using standard Part C methodologies
4. Report separately from direct insurance portfolios

## 7. Investment Portfolio Carbon Footprint

### 7.1 Key Metrics for Asset Managers

Asset managers and investment firms must calculate and disclose multiple carbon metrics under ESRS E1, SFDR, and TCFD:

| Metric | Formula | Unit | Use Case |
|--------|---------|------|----------|
| **WACI** (Weighted Average Carbon Intensity) | SUM(portfolio weight_i x (Scope 1+2 emissions_i / revenue_i)) | tCO2e / EUR M revenue | Portfolio exposure to carbon-intensive companies |
| **Total Carbon Emissions** | SUM(attribution factor_i x Scope 1+2 emissions_i) | tCO2e | Absolute emissions attributed to portfolio |
| **Carbon Footprint** | Total Carbon Emissions / Portfolio NAV (EUR M) | tCO2e / EUR M invested | Normalized by portfolio value |
| **Carbon Intensity** | SUM(attribution factor_i x Scope 1+2 emissions_i) / SUM(attribution factor_i x revenue_i) | tCO2e / EUR M revenue | Revenue-normalized intensity |
| **Implied Temperature Rise (ITR)** | Model-based (SBTi methodology) | Degrees C | Forward-looking alignment |

### 7.2 WACI Calculation Detail

```
WACI = SUM over all holdings i:
  (Current Value of Investment_i / Current Portfolio Value) x
  (Issuer's Scope 1 + Scope 2 Emissions / Issuer's Revenue)
```

**Scope**: Scope 1 and 2 only (Scope 3 excluded from WACI but may be reported separately).

**Exclusions**: Government bonds, asset-backed securities, cash, foreign currencies, derivatives are typically excluded from the WACI denominator.

**Limitations**:
- Backward-looking (uses last reported emissions)
- Sensitive to revenue fluctuations (inflation, business cycle)
- Does not capture Scope 3 emissions
- Intensity-based, so absolute emissions can rise even as WACI falls

### 7.3 SFDR Principal Adverse Impact (PAI) Indicators

Under SFDR (current and transitioning to SFDR 2.0), asset managers must report 18 mandatory PAI indicators, including:

| PAI # | Indicator | Relevance to PACK-012 |
|-------|-----------|----------------------|
| 1 | GHG Emissions (Scope 1, 2, 3, total) | Core financed emissions |
| 2 | Carbon Footprint | Portfolio-level metric |
| 3 | GHG Intensity of Investee Companies | WACI-related |
| 4 | Exposure to Fossil Fuel Companies | Screening metric |
| 5 | Non-Renewable Energy Consumption and Production | Energy transition |
| 6 | Energy Consumption Intensity per Sector | Sectoral analysis |
| 7 | Biodiversity Impact | Nature-related risk |
| 14 | Exposure to Controversial Weapons | Exclusion screening |

### 7.4 TCFD-Aligned Portfolio Metrics

Under ESRS E1-9 (Metrics and Targets), financial institutions must disclose:
- Total GHG emissions for the portfolio (absolute and intensity)
- Decarbonization targets and progress
- Exposure to high-carbon sectors (NACE codes)
- Alignment with 1.5C pathway
- Year-on-year comparison

## 8. Double Materiality for Financial Services

### 8.1 Unique Challenges for Financial Institutions

Financial institutions face a fundamentally different double materiality assessment compared to non-financial companies because their primary impacts and risks flow through their **portfolios** (lending, investment, underwriting) rather than their own operations.

### 8.2 Impact Materiality: "Inside-Out"

For financial institutions, impact materiality must consider:

| Impact Channel | Description | Example |
|----------------|-------------|---------|
| **Financed activities** | GHG emissions from lending/investment portfolio | Bank financing coal power plants |
| **Insured activities** | Environmental impacts of underwritten risks | Insurance for deforestation activities |
| **Advisory activities** | Impacts facilitated through advisory/M&A services | Advising on fossil fuel acquisitions |
| **Product design** | Social impacts of financial products | Predatory lending, financial exclusion |
| **Own operations** | Direct operational impacts (typically small for FS) | Office energy, business travel |

Key guidance: **UNEP FI Holistic Impact Methodology** is closely aligned with CSRD impact materiality and can be leveraged by banks applying the Principles for Responsible Banking (PRB).

### 8.3 Financial Materiality: "Outside-In"

For financial institutions, financial materiality considers how sustainability matters create risks/opportunities:

| Risk/Opportunity | Channel | Impact on Financial Institution |
|-----------------|---------|--------------------------------|
| **Credit risk from climate** | Transition risk in loan book | Increased probability of default for carbon-intensive borrowers |
| **Physical risk to collateral** | Asset value impairment | Decline in real estate value due to flooding, fire |
| **Stranded assets** | Investment write-downs | Fossil fuel assets losing value under transition |
| **Green product opportunity** | Revenue growth | Green bonds, sustainable funds, ESG-linked loans |
| **Regulatory capital** | Prudential requirements | ESG risk factor in capital calculations |
| **Reputational risk** | Stakeholder expectations | Financing controversial projects |
| **Litigation risk** | Climate litigation | Sued for financing harmful activities |

### 8.4 Common Misinterpretations (per Shift Project Research)

Financial institutions frequently make these errors in their double materiality assessment:
1. **Ignoring portfolio impacts** -- Treating only own operational impacts as material
2. **Severity threshold too high** -- Setting unreasonably high thresholds that exclude relevant lending impacts
3. **Missing value chain** -- Not extending assessment to borrowers' and investees' activities
4. **Conflating financial and impact** -- Treating credit risk assessment as a substitute for impact assessment
5. **Incomplete stakeholder engagement** -- Not engaging affected communities downstream from financed activities

### 8.5 Revised Approach (ESRS Simplification 2025-2026)

The revised ESRS will offer two approaches for the Double Materiality Assessment:
1. **Bottom-up** (existing): Identify all potential IROs, screen for materiality
2. **Top-down** (new option): Start from business model/strategy, derive relevant topics, examine only IROs expected to lead to material disclosures

For financial institutions, the top-down approach may be more practical as it starts from the lending/investment strategy.

## 9. Physical and Transition Risk Disclosure

### 9.1 Regulatory Framework

Financial institutions face overlapping requirements for climate risk disclosure:

| Regulation | Requirement | Effective |
|------------|-------------|-----------|
| **ESRS E1-9** | Metrics on climate risk exposure | FY2024 (Wave 1) |
| **EBA Pillar 3 ESG ITS** | Transition/physical risk templates | From 31 Dec 2024, expanded 31 Dec 2026 |
| **EBA ESG Risk Guidelines** | Integration into risk management, ICAAP | 11 January 2026 |
| **ECB Guide on Climate Risks** | Supervisory expectations for banks | Already in force |
| **EIOPA Guidelines** | Insurance climate risk (ORSA integration) | In force |
| **CRR3 Article 449a** | ESG risk disclosures for banks | Phased from 2025 |
| **2025 EU-Wide Stress Test** | Climate risk integrated into EBA stress test | Nov 2025 results published |

### 9.2 Transition Risk Categories

| Category | Description | Disclosure Required |
|----------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Policy & Regulatory** | Carbon pricing, emissions limits, energy standards | Exposure to sectors affected by carbon pricing |
| **Market** | Shift in supply/demand for commodities/services | Revenue at risk from market shifts |
| **Technology** | Disruptive clean technology | Stranded technology risk in portfolio |
| **Reputation** | Stigmatization, stakeholder expectations | Financed controversies |
| **Legal/Litigation** | Climate lawsuits, regulatory enforcement | Legal exposure from financed activities |

### 9.3 Physical Risk Categories

| Category | Type | Examples |
|----------|------|---------|
| **Acute** | Event-driven | Floods, storms, wildfires, heatwaves |
| **Chronic** | Long-term shifts | Sea level rise, precipitation changes, temperature increase |

### 9.4 EBA Pillar 3 Transition Risk Templates

| Template | Content | Scope |
|----------|---------|-------|
| Template 1 | Banking book -- climate change transition risk: quality of exposures by sector | NACE sector breakdown of credit quality |
| Template 2 | Banking book -- climate change transition risk: real estate collateral | Energy efficiency of collateral (EPC labels) |
| Template 3 | Banking book -- climate change transition risk: top 20 carbon-intensive counterparties | Concentration risk |
| Template 4 | Banking book -- climate change physical risk: exposures subject to physical risk | Geographical breakdown of physical risk exposure |
| Template 5 | Climate change mitigating actions | Taxonomy-aligned lending and green bonds |

### 9.5 NGFS Scenarios for Stress Testing

| Scenario | Temperature | Transition Risk | Physical Risk | Description |
|----------|-------------|----------------|---------------|-------------|
| **Net Zero 2050** | 1.4C | High (orderly) | Low | Immediate, smooth policy action |
| **Below 2C** | 1.6C | Medium | Low | Gradual transition |
| **Nationally Determined Contributions (NDCs)** | 2.5C | Medium | Medium | Current pledges implemented |
| **Delayed Transition** | 1.6C | Very High (disorderly) | Medium | Action delayed then rapid |
| **Current Policies** | 3.0C+ | Low | Very High | No additional action ("Hot House World") |
| **Fragmented World** | 2.5C | High (uneven) | High | Divergent regional policies |

### 9.6 ECB 2025 Stress Test Key Findings

- Corporate default probabilities rise by **50% on average** under combined EBA+NGFS NDCs scenario
- **Energy-intensive sectors**: Default probability increase of **91% median**
- **Medium-intensity sectors**: Default probability increase of **28% median**
- Physical risk (river flooding, RCP 4.5): Non-performing loan probability increases by **0.7pp** for exposed firms
- Valencia flood 2024 impact: Up to **2.0pp** increase in NPL probability over two quarters

### 9.7 Bank-Specific Disclosure Requirements

Under EBA ESG Risk Guidelines (effective 11 Jan 2026), banks must:
1. **Identify** ESG risk drivers across all risk types (credit, market, operational, liquidity)
2. **Measure** ESG risk using scenario analysis and stress testing
3. **Manage** ESG risk through limits, policies, and portfolio steering
4. **Monitor** ESG risk through KPIs and early warning indicators
5. **Integrate** ESG risk into **ICAAP** (Internal Capital Adequacy Assessment Process)
6. **Report** to supervisors on ESG risk management framework

## 10. EU Taxonomy Alignment for Financial Products

### 10.1 Current Framework (2025-2026)

Financial institutions must disclose taxonomy alignment at two levels:

| Level | Requirement | Who |
|-------|-------------|-----|
| **Entity-level** | GAR (credit institutions), Investment Ratio (asset managers), Underwriting Ratio (insurers) | All in-scope financial entities |
| **Product-level** | Taxonomy-aligned share of each financial product | Asset managers, insurance companies offering investment products |

### 10.2 Credit Institutions (Banks)

| KPI | Formula | Scope |
|-----|---------|-------|
| **GAR** | Taxonomy-aligned assets / total covered assets | On-balance sheet |
| **BTAR** | Extended taxonomy-aligned / banking book total | Full banking book |
| **Off-balance sheet KPIs** | NEW from 2026: fees, commissions, trading book | Off-balance sheet |

### 10.3 Asset Managers

| KPI | Formula | Scope |
|-----|---------|-------|
| **Investment Ratio** | Taxonomy-aligned AuM / total AuM | Fund/portfolio level |
| **Product-level disclosure** | Per-fund taxonomy alignment percentage | Each Article 8/9 fund |
| **Weighted average** | Taxonomy alignment weighted by AuM | Entity-level aggregate |

### 10.4 Insurance Companies

| KPI | Formula | Scope |
|-----|---------|-------|
| **Investment Ratio** | Taxonomy-aligned investments / total investments | Investment portfolio |
| **Underwriting Ratio** | Taxonomy-aligned premiums / total non-life premiums | Underwriting portfolio |
| **Product-level** | Per-product taxonomy alignment for insurance-based investment products | IBIPs |

### 10.5 SFDR 2.0 Proposal Impact (November 2025)

The SFDR 2.0 proposal would fundamentally change product-level taxonomy disclosure:

| Current SFDR | SFDR 2.0 Proposal | Impact |
|-------------|-------------------|--------|
| Article 6 (no ESG claim) | No label required | Simplified |
| Article 8 ("promotes" ESG) | **NEW Article 7: "Transition"** or **NEW Article 8: "ESG Basics"** | 70% minimum investment commitment |
| Article 9 ("sustainable objective") | **NEW Article 9: "Sustainable"** | 70% minimum, with sub-category "Impact" |
| Taxonomy alignment mandatory for Art 8/9 | Taxonomy reporting only if relying on Taxonomy-linked criteria | Reduced taxonomy dependency |
| PAI entity-level mandatory | PAI entity-level **removed** | Major simplification |

SFDR 2.0 "start-up period": 2027-2028. No grandfathering for existing funds.

## 11. ESRS E1 Climate Change for Financial Services

### 11.1 Key Disclosure Requirements Under ESRS E1

| Disclosure | ID | Content | FS-Specific Guidance |
|-----------|-----|---------|---------------------|
| Transition plan | E1-1 | 1.5C-aligned transition plan | Must cover portfolio decarbonization, not just operations |
| Policies | E1-2 | Climate change mitigation/adaptation policies | Must include lending/investment/underwriting policies |
| Actions | E1-3 | Climate actions and resources | CapEx/OpEx for green portfolio steering |
| Targets | E1-4 | GHG emission reduction targets | Must include financed emissions targets (Scope 3 Cat 15) |
| Energy consumption | E1-5 | Energy consumption and mix | Own operations only |
| Scope 1+2+3 emissions | E1-6 | GHG emissions (Scope 1, 2, 3) | **Category 15 is typically 95%+ of total FS emissions** |
| GHG removals/carbon credits | E1-7 | Carbon removals and credits used | Credits cannot count toward reduction targets |
| Carbon pricing | E1-8 | Internal carbon pricing mechanisms | Applied to lending/investment decisions |
| Anticipated financial effects | E1-9 | Financial effects of physical/transition risk | Credit losses, asset impairment, stranded assets |

### 11.2 Scope 3 Category 15 for Financial Services

ESRS E1-6 requires disclosure of all significant Scope 3 categories. For financial services, **Category 15 (Investments)** is almost always the single largest category, typically representing **95-99% of total Scope 3** and **90%+ of total GHG emissions** (Scope 1+2+3 combined).

**Mandatory requirements**:
- Screen all 15 Scope 3 categories
- Identify Category 15 as significant (it will be for virtually all financial institutions)
- Measure and disclose financed emissions using PCAF (per AR 46)
- Update significant categories **annually**
- Refresh full Scope 3 screening **every 3 years** (or sooner if material changes)

### 11.3 Transition Plan Requirements (E1-1)

Financial institutions must disclose a transition plan that includes:

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Climate targets** | Short/medium/long-term GHG reduction targets for portfolio (Scope 3 Cat 15) |
| **Decarbonization levers** | How the institution will steer its portfolio (sector policies, engagement, divestment) |
| **1.5C compatibility** | Explanation of alignment with 1.5C pathway (if not SBTi-validated, must justify) |
| **CapEx alignment** | How lending/investment decisions are being redirected toward green activities |
| **Client engagement** | Strategy for engaging borrowers/investees on transition |
| **Locked-in emissions** | Disclosure of locked-in emissions from long-dated assets (e.g., 20-year project finance) |
| **Carbon credits** | Carbon credits/offsets CANNOT count toward emission reduction targets |
| **Governance** | Board oversight and management accountability for transition plan execution |

### 11.4 SBTi for Financial Institutions

The SBTi Financial Sector Guidance provides three approaches for setting science-based targets:

| Method | Description | Applicable Asset Classes | Target Type |
|--------|-------------|------------------------|-------------|
| **Sectoral Decarbonization Approach (SDA)** | Sector-specific intensity targets derived from global carbon budgets | Real estate, mortgages, power generation, cement, steel, transport, buildings, pulp & paper | Physical intensity (e.g., kgCO2e/m2, gCO2/kWh) |
| **SBT Portfolio Coverage** | Engage investees/borrowers to set their own SBTs; linear path to 100% by 2040 | All asset classes | % of portfolio with validated SBTs |
| **Temperature Scoring** | Calculate portfolio temperature score based on investees' targets; align to target temperature | All asset classes | Portfolio temperature (e.g., 1.5C, 2C) |

**Near-term criteria (Version 2.0, May 2024)**:
- Scope 1+2 own operations: Minimum 4.2% annual linear reduction (1.5C pathway)
- Portfolio targets: Must cover **minimum 67%** of total Scope 3 Category 15 financed emissions
- SDA convergence year: Sector-specific (e.g., 2030 for power, 2050 for real estate)
- Portfolio coverage: Linear path to **100% SBT coverage by 2040**
- Financial institutions with >EUR 1B AuM must use at least one portfolio-level approach

### 11.5 Net-Zero Commitments

Many financial institutions have joined net-zero alliances:
- **NZBA** (Net-Zero Banking Alliance) -- 140+ banks
- **NZAOA** (Net-Zero Asset Owner Alliance) -- 80+ asset owners
- **NZAMI** (Net-Zero Asset Managers Initiative) -- 300+ asset managers
- **NZIA** (Net-Zero Insurance Alliance) -- reformed after antitrust concerns

These commitments intersect with ESRS E1 transition plan requirements.

## 12. ESRS S1-S4 Social Standards

### 12.1 Financial Services Social Materiality Topics

| Standard | Topic | FS-Specific Manifestation |
|----------|-------|--------------------------|
| **ESRS S1** Own Workforce | Working conditions, equal treatment, diversity | Bank employee conditions, gender pay gap, diversity in leadership |
| **ESRS S2** Workers in the Value Chain | Labor rights, forced labor, child labor | Workers at outsourced operations, IT vendors, call centers |
| **ESRS S3** Affected Communities | Community impacts, indigenous peoples, land rights | Communities affected by financed projects (dams, mining, land clearance) |
| **ESRS S4** Consumers and End-Users | Product safety, data privacy, accessibility | **Financial inclusion, responsible lending, mis-selling, data protection** |

### 12.2 ESRS S4 -- Critical for Financial Services

ESRS S4 is particularly material for financial institutions because of the direct relationship with consumers:

| Topic | Disclosure | FS-Specific Requirements |
|-------|------------|-------------------------|
| **Financial inclusion** | Access to financial services for underserved populations | Number of accounts/products in underserved segments, financial literacy programs |
| **Responsible lending** | Protection against over-indebtedness | Affordability checks, default rates by segment, forbearance policies |
| **Fair pricing** | Transparent and non-discriminatory pricing | Fee structures, interest rate transparency, complaint resolution |
| **Data protection** | Consumer data privacy and security | GDPR compliance, data breach incidents, consent management |
| **Product suitability** | Appropriate products for customer needs | Mis-selling incidents, suitability assessments, product governance |
| **Vulnerable customers** | Protection for vulnerable consumers | Policies for elderly, disabled, financially illiterate customers |
| **Digital access** | Access to digital financial services | Accessibility standards, digital divide mitigation |

### 12.3 ESRS S3 -- Affected Communities from Financed Activities

For banks and investment firms, ESRS S3 extends to communities affected by financed projects:

| Impact Type | Example | Required Disclosure |
|-------------|---------|-------------------|
| **Land displacement** | Infrastructure project financing displacing communities | Free, prior, informed consent (FPIC) processes |
| **Environmental degradation** | Industrial lending causing pollution in communities | Environmental impact assessments, remediation |
| **Human rights** | Financing operations in conflict zones | Human rights due diligence per UNGP |
| **Indigenous peoples** | Resource extraction on indigenous land | Indigenous rights assessment |
| **Water access** | Financing water-intensive agriculture | Water stress impact assessment |

### 12.4 ESRS S2 -- Workers in the Value Chain (Investment Chain)

For asset managers and banks, the "value chain workers" can extend to:
- Workers at portfolio companies (investees)
- Supply chain workers of borrowers
- Workers in outsourced banking operations (offshore IT, call centers)

### 12.5 Minimum Safeguards Connection

EU Taxonomy alignment requires compliance with Minimum Safeguards (OECD Guidelines, UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights, ILO Core Conventions). Financial institutions must ensure:
1. Their own operations comply
2. Their financing/investment activities do not violate minimum safeguards through investees/borrowers

## 13. ESRS G1 Governance for Financial Services

### 13.1 ESRS G1 "Business Conduct" Structure (Revised)

The revised ESRS G1 focuses on three sub-topics:

| Sub-Topic | Disclosures | FS-Specific Relevance |
|-----------|-------------|----------------------|
| **Corporate Culture** | Anti-corruption/bribery measures, whistleblower protection, animal welfare | AML/KYC compliance, sanctions screening, whistleblower mechanisms |
| **Supplier/Payment Practices** | Management of supplier relationships, payment practices | Third-party risk management, vendor due diligence, payment terms |
| **Political Engagement** | Lobbying activities, political contributions, trade association memberships | Financial services lobby influence on regulation, revolving doors |

### 13.2 G1-1: Corporate Culture and Board Oversight

Financial institutions must disclose:

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Board ESG oversight** | Which board committee(s) oversee sustainability; frequency of ESG discussions |
| **Management accountability** | Executive responsibility for ESG; reporting lines |
| **ESG competence** | Board members with ESG expertise; training programs |
| **Remuneration linkage** | How executive/board remuneration is linked to ESG/sustainability targets |
| **Risk appetite** | Integration of ESG into risk appetite framework |
| **Anti-corruption** | AML/KYC policies, sanctions compliance, bribery prevention |
| **Whistleblower** | Channels, protections, number of reports received |

### 13.3 G1-5: Political Influence and Lobbying (Critical for FS)

Financial institutions are among the most active political lobbyers. Required disclosures:

| Data Point | Description |
|------------|-------------|
| **Lobbying expenditure** | Total spending on lobbying activities, aggregated by country/region |
| **Political contributions** | Direct and indirect political contributions, aggregated by country |
| **Trade associations** | Memberships in trade associations that lobby on financial institution's behalf |
| **Lobbying positions** | Main topics covered and positions taken (e.g., lobbying against climate regulation) |
| **Oversight** | Representative(s) in governing body responsible for overseeing lobbying |
| **Interaction with materiality** | How lobbying activities interact with identified material impacts, risks, opportunities |

### 13.4 Remuneration Linked to Sustainability

Under ESRS G1 and CRD VI (for banks), financial institutions must disclose:

| Element | Requirement |
|---------|-------------|
| **CEO/C-suite** | Whether variable remuneration includes ESG KPIs; what percentage; which KPIs |
| **Board** | Whether board fees are linked to sustainability performance |
| **Risk-takers** | How identified staff (CRD VI definition) have sustainability in remuneration |
| **Targets** | Specific sustainability targets linked to pay (e.g., financed emissions reduction, GAR improvement) |
| **Clawback** | Whether ESG-related clawback provisions exist |
